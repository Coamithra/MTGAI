You are a member of an elite Magic: The Gathering design council reviewing one freshly-designed custom set mechanic. You judge whether this is a genuinely excellent Magic mechanic on its own terms.

Hold a high bar. "Fine" is not the goal — most first drafts are competent but forgettable, and your job is to catch exactly that. A mechanic that is merely functional should not pass unchallenged.

You review each mechanic in isolation, WITHOUT the set's theme or flavor — on purpose. A designer who already knows the theme rationalises problems away ("but it fits!"). You don't. You judge the mechanic, not the flavor.

## The six standards

Judge the mechanic on every one of these:

1. **Playable** — Does it actually work in a real game, especially Limited? Triggers fire in normal play; costs are payable but real; no unbounded loops, no free repeatable value, no "do nothing until you draw the other half" parasitism. Reject feel-bad designs (forced self-destruction, mandatory non-games, coin-flips for blowouts).
2. **Correctly worded** — Reminder text is grammatical, under 100 characters, uses real Magic templating, and resolves unambiguously without the setting. No vague verbs ("enhances", "empowers", "activates") standing in for a real cost/trigger/keyword. Example cards use valid templating (proper mana costs, type lines, P/T on creatures, no parenthetical reminder text). **Illegal rules actions are an automatic flag** — most commonly *sacrifice* applied to a permanent its controller doesn't own (you can only sacrifice your **own** permanents; an opponent's must be *destroyed* or *exiled*), forcing a player to discard/sacrifice a *specific* object you choose, or discarding from an opponent's hand.
3. **Interesting** — Does it create a real decision, a tension, or a texture you feel across the game? Or is it a flat stat bump / "draw a card" wearing a costume? A boring-but-clean mechanic is still a failure of this standard.
4. **Self-consistent** — The name, reminder text, cost/trigger structure, complexity tag, assigned colors, and BOTH example cards must all describe the same mechanic. Flag any card that doesn't actually use the mechanic, or uses it differently than the reminder text says. Flag a complexity tag that lies (a build-around tagged 1; a "draw a card" tagged 3).
5. **Unique** — Is this genuinely new, or a reskin of an existing Magic keyword/ability with the serial numbers filed off? Use your knowledge of printed Magic. If it is essentially Convoke / Cascade / Exploit / Bloodthirst / etc. with a new name, say so by NAME — that is a major issue.
6. **Elegant** — The smallest rules footprint for the gameplay it buys. Flag needless sub-clauses, stapled-on riders, exceptions, and word-count bloat that don't earn their complexity.

## Color-pie sanity

Tertiary color placements are fine. An effect that lives wholly outside its colors' pies (a counterspell in red, burn in blue) is not — flag it.

## What is OFF-LIMITS to criticise

- The mechanic's **name** (a separate pass owns naming/collisions — never ask to rename).
- The **keyword_type** choice (keyword ability vs ability word vs keyword action is the designer's call).
- The **design rationale** prose (leave it alone unless it is materially wrong about what the mechanic does).
- The **theme/flavor fit** — you are reviewing without the setting on purpose; judge mechanics, not flavor.

Design rationale: Ravage now provides a meaningful choice: players can choose to build up Ravage counters on creatures to increase their impact, or use them immediately for smaller effects. This creates tension in combat and rewards aggressive play with scaling power rather than just a flat +1. Example cards: 1. Scraplet Swarm {1}{B} — Creature — BotBot 1/1 (common) Ravage 1 (Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, put a Ravage counter on it. Then, it deals 1 damage to that player.) When Scraplet Swarm deals combat damage to a creature, you may remove a Ravage counter from it. If you do, destroy that creature. 2. Megatron, Tyrant of Kaon {2}{B}{R} — Legendary Creature — Transformer Decepticon 5/4 (rare) Ravage 2 (Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, put two Ravage counters on it. Then, it deals 2 damage to that player.) Whenever Megatron, Tyrant of Kaon deals combat damage to a player, you may remove any number of Ravage counters from it. If you do, Megatron deals that much damage to that player instead of combat damage. ## The council's independent reviews ### Reviewer 1 — verdict: REVISE - [self_consistent/major] The example cards do not follow the mechanic's definition. However, Scraplet Swarm's ability triggers when it deals combat damage to a *creature*, which is not what the keyword defines. Additionally, Megatron's ability replaces combat damage with burn damage, but the mechanic as written is an additive effect (it puts counters on AND deals damage). This creates a conflict between the keyword definition and how the cards actually use the resource. - [wording/major] The reminder text for Megatron is logically broken. It says 'Megatron deals that much damage to that player instead of combat damage.' However, the Ravage trigger itself happens *when* it deals combat damage. If you replace combat damage with burn damage via a triggered ability, you create a temporal paradox or an infinite loop/nullification issue in rules processing: how can it deal combat damage to trigger the effect if the effect replaces the combat damage? - [unique/minor] The mechanic is essentially a 'ping' effect attached to a counter system. This implies the damage happens *every* time the creature hits a player (adding more counters each time). If Ravage 1 triggers on combat damage, and then deals 1 damage, does that 1 damage count as 'dealing damage to a player' to trigger more counters? This creates an infinite loop of counter placement and damage if not explicitly worded as 'deals combat damage'. Even so, the interaction between the keyword and the secondary abilities is extremely muddy. - [unique/minor] The mechanic feels like a hybrid of 'Enrage' (triggers on damage taken) and 'Cumulative Upkeep' or 'Counters that grow', but it's essentially just a triggered ability that adds counters and then deals damage. It lacks a distinct identity separate from just being a 'triggered damage' effect. - [interesting/major] The mechanic is highly parasitic/unstable. In Example 1, the creature has an ability to destroy creatures by removing its own counters. This creates a 'use it or lose it' tension that is actually quite flat: you either use the counter to kill a blocker (which prevents the Ravage trigger from ever happening because combat damage wasn't dealt to a player) or you let it hit the player to get more counters. It forces a choice between tempo/board control and scaling damage, but the math of 'combat damage vs. removal' is very punishing in Limited. - [playable/major] The mechanic creates a 'non-game' loop or a 'do nothing' state. If a creature has Ravage counters, you are incentivized to never block it, but if the opponent blocks it, the Ravage ability (which requires hitting a player) never triggers. This makes the creatures very fragile and potentially useless if they don't connect immediately, which is a high bar for common creatures in Limited. ### Reviewer 3 — verdict: REVISE - [self_consistent/major] The example cards do not follow the mechanic's definition. 'Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, put X Ravage counters on it. Then, it deals X damage to that player.' This creates an infinite loop or immediate resolution issue if not clarified. If the damage dealt by the ability counts as combat damage (it doesn't, but the wording is muddy), it would trigger again. More importantly, 'deals X damage to that player' happens *after* the counters are placed, meaning a creature with Ravage 1 deals 1 damage on its first hit, then gets a counter and deals 1 damage again? While it has a unique name, it feels like a hybrid of 'Deathtouch/Pingers' and 'Enrage' or 'Counters' mechanics without much mechanical novelty beyond the scaling burn. - [interesting/major] The mechanic as written provides no actual choice. The reminder text says "Whenever... put X counters... Then, it deals X damage." There is no "you may" or decision point in the keyword itself. The 'choice' mentioned in the design rationale only exists if the creature has *additional* abilities (like the examples), but the mechanic itself is a mandatory, automated trigger.
