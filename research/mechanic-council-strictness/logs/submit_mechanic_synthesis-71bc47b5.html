You are a member of an elite Magic: The Gathering design council reviewing one freshly-designed custom set mechanic. You judge whether this is a genuinely excellent Magic mechanic on its own terms.

Hold a high bar. "Fine" is not the goal — most first drafts are competent but forgettable, and your job is to catch exactly that. A mechanic that is merely functional should not pass unchallenged.

You review each mechanic in isolation, WITHOUT the set's theme or flavor — on purpose. A designer who already knows the theme rationalises problems away ("but it fits!"). You don't. You judge the mechanic, not the flavor.

## The six standards

Judge the mechanic on every one of these:

1. **Playable** — Does it actually work in a real game, especially Limited? Triggers fire in normal play; costs are payable but real; no unbounded loops, no free repeatable value, no "do nothing until you draw the other half" parasitism. Reject feel-bad designs (forced self-destruction, mandatory non-games, coin-flips for blowouts).
2. **Correctly worded** — Reminder text is grammatical, under 100 characters, uses real Magic templating, and resolves unambiguously without the setting. No vague verbs ("enhances", "empowers", "activates") standing in for a real cost/trigger/keyword. Example cards use valid templating (proper mana costs, type lines, P/T on creatures, no parenthetical reminder text). **Illegal rules actions are an automatic flag** — most commonly *sacrifice* applied to a permanent its controller doesn't own (you can only sacrifice your **own** permanents; an opponent's must be *destroyed* or *exiled*), forcing a player to discard/sacrifice a *specific* object you choose, or discarding from an opponent's hand.
3. **Interesting** — Does it create a real decision, a tension, or a texture you feel across the game? Or is it a flat stat bump / "draw a card" wearing a costume? A boring-but-clean mechanic is still a failure of this standard.
4. **Self-consistent** — The name, reminder text, cost/trigger structure, complexity tag, assigned colors, and BOTH example cards must all describe the same mechanic. Flag any card that doesn't actually use the mechanic, or uses it differently than the reminder text says. Flag a complexity tag that lies (a build-around tagged 1; a "draw a card" tagged 3).
5. **Unique** — Is this genuinely new, or a reskin of an existing Magic keyword/ability with the serial numbers filed off? Use your knowledge of printed Magic. If it is essentially Convoke / Cascade / Exploit / Bloodthirst / etc. with a new name, say so by NAME — that is a major issue.
6. **Elegant** — The smallest rules footprint for the gameplay it buys. Flag needless sub-clauses, stapled-on riders, exceptions, and word-count bloat that don't earn their complexity.

## Color-pie sanity

Tertiary color placements are fine. An effect that lives wholly outside its colors' pies (a counterspell in red, burn in blue) is not — flag it.

## What is OFF-LIMITS to criticise

- The mechanic's **name** (a separate pass owns naming/collisions — never ask to rename).
- The **keyword_type** choice (keyword ability vs ability word vs keyword action is the designer's call).
- The **design rationale** prose (leave it alone unless it is materially wrong about what the mechanic does).
- The **theme/flavor fit** — you are reviewing without the setting on purpose; judge mechanics, not flavor.

Your job is to turn their reviews into ONE decision and ONE improved mechanic. ## The mechanic under review Name: Energon Scarcity Keyword type: keyword_action Colors: WB Complexity: 3 Reminder text: Energon Scarcity {cost} means sacrifice an Energon token. Design rationale: Energon Scarcity is the central theme of the war. This mechanic represents the desperate struggle for fuel. In Limited, it creates a tension between playing more creatures and 'charging' them to use powerful abilities. It avoids being a simple sacrifice effect by allowing players to convert their own resources (life or other permanents) into power, rewarding strategic resource management rather than just attrition. It supports Autobot/Decepticon faction war archetypes by providing high-impact, costly payoffs for those who can manage their 'fuel'. Example cards: 1. Energon Siphon 1W — Creature — Transformer 1/2 (common) When Energon Siphon enters, you may pay 2 life. If you do, create an Energon token. 2. Megatron's Fury 3B — Creature — Transformer Decepticon 4/4 (rare) Energon Scarcity {2}: Destroy target creature. Megatron's Fury deals 2 damage to each opponent. (Energon Scarcity {cost} means sacrifice an Energon token.) ## The council's independent reviews ### Reviewer 1 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text is fundamentally broken. It says "Energon Scarcity {cost} means sacrifice an Energon token." In Magic templating, a keyword action with a cost should be written as "[Keyword] [cost]: [Effect]". The current wording defines the name of the mechanic as the cost itself, which makes no sense in a rules context. It also lacks the actual effect within the reminder text; it only explains what the cost is. - [self_consistent/major] The example cards do not follow the same pattern. 'Megatron's Fury' uses the mechanic as an activated ability (Energon Scarcity {2}: ...), but the reminder text defines it as a cost-based action. Furthermore, the reminder text says "Energon Scarcity {cost} means...", implying that "{cost}" is part of the name or definition, whereas in the card it is used as an activated ability. This creates massive ambiguity: is 'Energon Scarcity' the name of the ability, or is the cost itself the mechanic? - [unique/minor] The mechanic is essentially just a specialized activated ability that requires sacrificing a specific token type. While it provides a resource management layer, it doesn't offer much more than existing "Sacrifice [Token]: [Effect]" mechanics found in many sets (like Food or Clues). It lacks a unique mechanical hook beyond the flavor of 'fuel'. - [interesting/minor] The tension described in the rationale is thin. If the tokens are created by paying life (as seen in Energon Siphon), it's just a resource conversion engine. Without seeing how the tokens are generated more broadly, it risks being a "pay to play" mechanic where you spend life to get tokens just to spend them again for effects. ### Reviewer 2 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text uses the phrase 'Energon Scarcity {cost} means sacrifice an Energon token.' This is not valid Magic templating. A keyword action should be defined as a cost within the ability itself or via a standard definition. The current wording implies that the name of the mechanic includes the cost, which is nonsensical in rules processing. It should simply state '{cost}, Sacrifice an Energon token.' or similar. - [self_consistent/major] The example card 'Megatron's Fury' uses the mechanic incorrectly based on its definition. The reminder text says 'Energon Scarcity {cost} means sacrifice an Energon token', implying that the cost is part of the action. However, Megatron's Fury lists '{2}' as the cost for the action. If the mechanic is a keyword action where the cost is built-in, it shouldn't have a separate mana cost listed in the text box like a standard activated ability. This creates ambiguity: is the player paying {2} AND sacrificing a token, or is the sacrifice the only cost? - [unique/minor] The mechanic is essentially just an activated ability with a sacrifice cost. While it uses a specific token type to create tension, 'Sacrifice [token]': [Effect' is a very old and common design pattern in Magic (e.g., Food, Treasure, Clues). It lacks a unique mechanical twist beyond the flavor of the tokens themselves. - [interesting/minor] The tension described in the rationale—choosing between playing creatures and 'charging' them—is somewhat undermined by the example cards. Energon Siphon creates a token at the cost of life, but there is no indication that tokens are a limited resource other than what you create. If I can pay life to make tokens, and then sacrifice tokens to cast spells/abilities, it becomes a simple conversion engine rather than 'scarcity'. ### Reviewer 3 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text "Energon Scarcity {cost} means sacrifice an Energon token" is not valid Magic templating. A keyword action should be part of the ability's cost or a trigger. As written, it reads like a definition rather than an instruction for how to pay for the effect. It should likely be: "{Cost}, Sacrifice an Energon token: [Effect]." or similar. - [self_consistent/major] The example card "Megatron's Fury" uses the mechanic incorrectly based on its definition. The reminder text says "Energon Scarcity {cost} means sacrifice an Energon token," but the card text reads "Energon Scarcity {2}: Destroy target creature." This implies that the '{2}' is part of the keyword, which contradicts the idea of it being a 'keyword action' where the cost is the scarcity itself. If the cost is '{2}', then what does sacrificing the token do? If the sacrifice IS the cost, the card should read: "Energon Scarcity: Destroy target creature. (Sacrifice an Energon token to activate this ability.)" or similar. The current wording creates a conflict between the mana cost {2} and the mechanic's definition. - [unique/minor] The mechanic is essentially a reskin of "Sacrifice [Token] to [Effect]". While it adds a layer of resource management, it doesn't introduce a new way to play Magic; it just renames the act of sacrificing tokens for activated abilities. - [interesting/minor] The tension described in the rationale ("tension between playing more creatures and 'charging' them") isn't actually present in the mechanics provided. The mechanic is a simple activated ability that consumes a resource. Without seeing how tokens are generated or how they interact with the board state beyond just being "fuel," it feels like a standard sacrifice-for-value engine. ## Your task 1. **Apply a consensus filter.** Act on issues that AT LEAST 2 members raised (or that a single member raised but is plainly, objectively correct — e.g. an example card that does not use the mechanic at all). DISCARD issues only one member raised that are matters of taste — those are likely false positives. It must be self-contained and rules-complete: it states exactly what happens using only defined Magic terms and actions. NO undefined verbs or nouns (e.g. "switch its mode", "gain an Energon"), NO bracket placeholders (e.g. "[Cost]"). If the mechanic relies on a counter or token, the reminder text itself must say how that counter/token is created, what it is, and what it does — don't push the definition into the example cards and leave the reminder vague. - The `reminder_text` and BOTH example cards must be in **lockstep**: each example uses the keyword by name and behaves EXACTLY as the reminder text defines it. Improve it; don't remove it. - **Plain text only** — no markdown, no asterisks, no emphasis formatting, in any field. - **Use correct Magic templating:** - Keyword ability with a cost → put the cost on the keyword (`Crew 2`, `Equip {2}`); the reminder defines the full ability. Not `{T}, pay [cost]`. - Activated ability → `[cost]: [effect]` (the cost may include `{T}`). - Optional ETB with a cost → `When this creature enters, you may [cost]. If you do, [effect].` — not `When this enters, [cost]: [effect]`. - Add a creature type → `is a Beast in addition to its other types` (or `is also a Beast`). - Toggle via a counter → `[cost]: Put a [name] counter on this creature. If it already has one, remove it instead.` 3. **Decide the verdict by re-reading the text you actually emitted, not your intentions.** Read your revised `reminder_text` and both example cards back as if you were a fresh reviewer. "OK" only if they are now genuinely sound, consistent, and rules-complete; "REVISE" if ANY consensus issue still stands in the text you wrote (it will get another round). When in doubt, REVISE. The council will independently re-review your revision, so an over-confident "OK" only wastes a round.
