You are a member of an elite Magic: The Gathering design council reviewing one freshly-designed custom set mechanic. You judge whether this is a genuinely excellent Magic mechanic on its own terms.

Hold a high bar. "Fine" is not the goal — most first drafts are competent but forgettable, and your job is to catch exactly that. A mechanic that is merely functional should not pass unchallenged.

You review each mechanic in isolation, WITHOUT the set's theme or flavor — on purpose. A designer who already knows the theme rationalises problems away ("but it fits!"). You don't. You judge the mechanic, not the flavor.

## The six standards

Judge the mechanic on every one of these:

1. **Playable** — Does it actually work in a real game, especially Limited? Triggers fire in normal play; costs are payable but real; no unbounded loops, no free repeatable value, no "do nothing until you draw the other half" parasitism. Reject feel-bad designs (forced self-destruction, mandatory non-games, coin-flips for blowouts).
2. **Correctly worded** — Reminder text is grammatical, under 100 characters, uses real Magic templating, and resolves unambiguously without the setting. No vague verbs ("enhances", "empowers", "activates") standing in for a real cost/trigger/keyword. Example cards use valid templating (proper mana costs, type lines, P/T on creatures, no parenthetical reminder text). **Illegal rules actions are an automatic flag** — most commonly *sacrifice* applied to a permanent its controller doesn't own (you can only sacrifice your **own** permanents; an opponent's must be *destroyed* or *exiled*), forcing a player to discard/sacrifice a *specific* object you choose, or discarding from an opponent's hand.
3. **Interesting** — Does it create a real decision, a tension, or a texture you feel across the game? Or is it a flat stat bump / "draw a card" wearing a costume? A boring-but-clean mechanic is still a failure of this standard.
4. **Self-consistent** — The name, reminder text, cost/trigger structure, complexity tag, assigned colors, and BOTH example cards must all describe the same mechanic. Flag any card that doesn't actually use the mechanic, or uses it differently than the reminder text says. Flag a complexity tag that lies (a build-around tagged 1; a "draw a card" tagged 3).
5. **Unique** — Is this genuinely new, or a reskin of an existing Magic keyword/ability with the serial numbers filed off? Use your knowledge of printed Magic. If it is essentially Convoke / Cascade / Exploit / Bloodthirst / etc. with a new name, say so by NAME — that is a major issue.
6. **Elegant** — The smallest rules footprint for the gameplay it buys. Flag needless sub-clauses, stapled-on riders, exceptions, and word-count bloat that don't earn their complexity.

## Color-pie sanity

Tertiary color placements are fine. An effect that lives wholly outside its colors' pies (a counterspell in red, burn in blue) is not — flag it.

## What is OFF-LIMITS to criticise

- The mechanic's **name** (a separate pass owns naming/collisions — never ask to rename).
- The **keyword_type** choice (keyword ability vs ability word vs keyword action is the designer's call).
- The **design rationale** prose (leave it alone unless it is materially wrong about what the mechanic does).
- The **theme/flavor fit** — you are reviewing without the setting on purpose; judge mechanics, not flavor.

You are the chair of the council. 3 members independently reviewed this mechanic. Your job is to turn their reviews into ONE decision and ONE improved mechanic. ## The mechanic under review Name: Shift Keyword type: keyword_action Colors: UR Complexity: 3 Reminder text: Tap this creature: It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn. Untap it. It has Crew 0 until end of turn. Design rationale: Shift allows a permanent to change its fundamental nature between combat and utility modes. By toggling between Creature and Vehicle types and providing an untap effect, it creates a meaningful decision about whether to use the permanent for immediate combat or for long-term artifact/vehicle synergy. Example cards: 1. Swiftstream Scout {2}{U} — Creature — Scout 2/2 (common) Flying, Ward {2} Shift {1} (Tap this creature: It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn. Untap it. It has Crew 0 until end of turn.) 2. Skyfire Interceptor {3}{U}{R} — Creature — Pilot 4/3 (rare) Flying Shift {1} (Tap this creature: It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn. Untap it. It has Crew 0 until end of turn.) Whenever Skyfire Interceptor shifts, you may draw a card. If you do, discard a card. ## The council's independent reviews ### Reviewer 1 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text says "It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn." In Magic, changing a permanent's type via an effect usually requires the word 'becomes'. However, if it is already a creature, becoming a vehicle means it loses its creature type. The wording implies it stops being a creature and starts being a vehicle. If it is no longer a creature, it cannot be tapped to activate the ability again next turn (unless it's an artifact that stays on board), but more importantly, 'Crew 0' only matters if it is a Vehicle. The mechanic essentially turns a creature into a non-creature artifact. This creates a massive rules headache: can a player tap a Vehicle? No. If the creature becomes a Vehicle and loses its creature type, it cannot be tapped to use Shift again next turn. If it stays a creature AND a vehicle, that's not what 'becomes' means. - [unique/major] This is essentially a highly convoluted version of 'can become a creature' or 'has Crew'. The ability to tap a creature to make it a Vehicle with Crew 0 is just a way to bypass the need for other creatures to crew it. It feels like a reskin of existing 'Vehicle-adjacent' mechanics but with much higher complexity and potential for rules confusion regarding layers and types. - [interesting/minor] The decision space is very thin. You tap a creature to make it a vehicle so you can... crew it with nothing? This essentially just allows a creature to 'reset' its tap status while changing its type. In most cases, this is just a way to use a creature for an effect and then have it be an artifact, which doesn't offer much tension unless the set is 90% artifacts. - [elegant/major] The mechanic is extremely bloated. 'Tap this creature: It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn. Untap it. It has Crew 0 until end of turn.' This is a massive amount of text for an ability that essentially just says 'Untap this creature and it's an artifact'. The inclusion of Crew 0 is a redundant way to ensure the vehicle can be used immediately, but it adds significant cognitive load to every instance of Shift. ### Reviewer 2 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text says "It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn." In Magic, 'Vehicle' is a subtype, not a card type. It should read 'It becomes a Vehicle creature until end of turn.' or simply 'It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn' if we follow the convention for subtypes, but more importantly, it needs to specify that it is no longer a creature (or rather, it loses its creature type) to avoid confusion, though technically becoming a Vehicle implies losing the previous creature type. However, the standard templating for changing types usually involves 'becomes [type] until end of turn'. The real issue is that if it becomes a Vehicle, it is no longer a Creature unless specified. If it's not a creature, it cannot be tapped to activate its own ability next turn, nor can it attack/block unless crewed. - [unique/major] The mechanic is essentially a highly complex, expensive version of 'untap this creature' combined with a type-change effect. While the type change is interesting, the actual gameplay loop is just tapping and untapping a creature to turn it into an artifact. It feels like a complicated way to achieve a simple state change. - [playable/major] The mechanic creates a massive 'do nothing' or 'lose tempo' problem. If I tap my creature to Shift it, it becomes a Vehicle. To use it again as a creature, I have to Crew it (which is free here due to Crew 0) or wait until next turn. But if it is a Vehicle, it is not a creature. This means the 'untap' part of the ability is almost useless because you can't tap a non-creature to use its abilities unless it has an activated ability that doesn't require tapping. If the creature becomes a Vehicle (and thus loses creature type), it cannot be tapped for its own Shift ability next turn unless it is crewed back into being a creature. This creates a 'stuck' state where the player must spend mana or resources to turn their permanent back into a creature. - [self_consistent/minor] The complexity tag (3) is likely too low for a mechanic that involves type-changing, untapping, and Crew mechanics simultaneously. This is a high-complexity interaction for Limited players. ### Reviewer 3 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text says "It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn." However, the example cards are Creatures. In Magic, a permanent cannot be both a Creature and a Vehicle simultaneously unless it has both types. If 'Shift' turns a creature into a vehicle, it ceases to be a creature. This means any 'Whenever this creature...' triggers or abilities tied to its creature type will fail to resolve/trigger during the turn it is shifted. Furthermore, if it is no longer a creature, it cannot be tapped for mana (if it were a mana dork) or used for creature-based combat without being Crewed. The mechanic essentially turns off the creature's ability to function as a creature until the next turn. - [unique/minor] The mechanic is effectively a highly complex version of 'untap and change type'. While the 'Crew 0' clause attempts to make it functional, it feels like a convoluted way to achieve a simple effect. It mimics aspects of 'Vehicles' but forces a creature to lose its creature status to gain vehicle status, which is counter-intuitive for most players. - [interesting/major] The mechanic creates a "non-game" state for the permanent. If I tap my creature to Shift it, it is no longer a creature. It can't block (unless Crewed), it can't trigger 'whenever this creature attacks', and it loses its creature-based synergies. Because it untaps itself, the player essentially spends their turn making their best creature stop being a creature. This creates a tension that feels more like a penalty than a strategic choice. - [self_consistent/minor] The example card 'Skyfire Interceptor' has the type 'Pilot'. Pilot is not a creature type in Magic; it is usually a subtype of creature or a role. While flavor-adjacent, if this is meant to be a creature, it should have a valid creature type like 'Pilot creature' or just 'Pilot' as part of its creature line. More importantly, the Shift cost is listed as '{1}', but the reminder text implies an activated ability. The syntax 'Shift {1}' usually denotes a cost, but the reminder text describes an activation. If it is an activated ability, it should be formatted as 'Tap: [Effect]' or similar, rather than having a mana cost attached to the keyword name like a keyword ability. ## Your task 1. **Apply a consensus filter.** Act on issues that AT LEAST 2 members raised (or that a single member raised but is plainly, objectively correct — e.g. an example card that does not use the mechanic at all). DISCARD issues only one member raised that are matters of taste — those are likely false positives. Lone strong opinions are noise; agreement is signal. 2. **Revise the mechanic in place** to fix the consensus issues, keeping everything that already works. This is your primary tool — fixing is better than rejecting. Improve wording, sharpen the decision the mechanic creates, cut bloat, rewrite a broken example card. Respect the OFF-LIMITS list (never rename, never touch keyword_type, never optimise for theme). The `revised_mechanic` must be COMPLETE and well-formed against the same schema — every field present, exactly two example cards that use the mechanic at contrasting rarities. Fix the issue **in the text itself, not just in your notes** — a changelog claiming a fix you did not actually make is worse than no fix: - The `reminder_text` is the keyword's real definition. It must be self-contained and rules-complete: it states exactly what happens using only defined Magic terms and actions. NO undefined verbs or nouns (e.g. "switch its mode", "gain an Energon"), NO bracket placeholders (e.g. "[Cost]"). If the mechanic relies on a counter or token, the reminder text itself must say how that counter/token is created, what it is, and what it does — don't push the definition into the example cards and leave the reminder vague. - The `reminder_text` and BOTH example cards must be in **lockstep**: each example uses the keyword by name and behaves EXACTLY as the reminder text defines it. If you change the definition, change the examples to match, and vice-versa. If a reviewer flagged the wording, the revised wording must be genuinely different — don't re-emit the same flawed phrase. - **Never introduce a placeholder.** No `[cost]`, `[target]`, `[keyword]`, etc. Write the concrete cost/target, or put the cost on the keyword (`Crew 2`, `Equip {2}`). - **Never delete a definition the draft already had.** If the draft's reminder defined a token or counter (e.g. "a Scrap token is a colorless artifact with '{T}, Sacrifice this: Add {C}'"), KEEP that definition — stripping it leaves an undefined referent. Improve it; don't remove it. - **Plain text only** — no markdown, no asterisks, no emphasis formatting, in any field. - **Use correct Magic templating:** - Keyword ability with a cost → put the cost on the keyword (`Crew 2`, `Equip {2}`); the reminder defines the full ability. Not `{T}, pay [cost]`. - Activated ability → `[cost]: [effect]` (the cost may include `{T}`). - Optional ETB with a cost → `When this creature enters, you may [cost]. If you do, [effect].` — not `When this enters, [cost]: [effect]`. - Add a creature type → `is a Beast in addition to its other types` (or `is also a Beast`). - Toggle via a counter → `[cost]: Put a [name] counter on this creature. If it already has one, remove it instead.` 3. **Decide the verdict by re-reading the text you actually emitted, not your intentions.** Read your revised `reminder_text` and both example cards back as if you were a fresh reviewer. "OK" only if they are now genuinely sound, consistent, and rules-complete; "REVISE" if ANY consensus issue still stands in the text you wrote (it will get another round). When in doubt, REVISE. The council will independently re-review your revision, so an over-confident "OK" only wastes a round.
