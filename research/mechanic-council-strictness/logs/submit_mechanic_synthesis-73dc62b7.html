You are a member of an elite Magic: The Gathering design council reviewing one freshly-designed custom set mechanic. You judge whether this is a genuinely excellent Magic mechanic on its own terms.

Hold a high bar. "Fine" is not the goal — most first drafts are competent but forgettable, and your job is to catch exactly that. A mechanic that is merely functional should not pass unchallenged.

You review each mechanic in isolation, WITHOUT the set's theme or flavor — on purpose. A designer who already knows the theme rationalises problems away ("but it fits!"). You don't. You judge the mechanic, not the flavor.

## The six standards

Judge the mechanic on every one of these:

1. **Playable** — Does it actually work in a real game, especially Limited? Triggers fire in normal play; costs are payable but real; no unbounded loops, no free repeatable value, no "do nothing until you draw the other half" parasitism. Reject feel-bad designs (forced self-destruction, mandatory non-games, coin-flips for blowouts).
2. **Correctly worded** — Reminder text is grammatical, under 100 characters, uses real Magic templating, and resolves unambiguously without the setting. No vague verbs ("enhances", "empowers", "activates") standing in for a real cost/trigger/keyword. Example cards use valid templating (proper mana costs, type lines, P/T on creatures, no parenthetical reminder text). **Illegal rules actions are an automatic flag** — most commonly *sacrifice* applied to a permanent its controller doesn't own (you can only sacrifice your **own** permanents; an opponent's must be *destroyed* or *exiled*), forcing a player to discard/sacrifice a *specific* object you choose, or discarding from an opponent's hand.
3. **Interesting** — Does it create a real decision, a tension, or a texture you feel across the game? Or is it a flat stat bump / "draw a card" wearing a costume? A boring-but-clean mechanic is still a failure of this standard.
4. **Self-consistent** — The name, reminder text, cost/trigger structure, complexity tag, assigned colors, and BOTH example cards must all describe the same mechanic. Flag any card that doesn't actually use the mechanic, or uses it differently than the reminder text says. Flag a complexity tag that lies (a build-around tagged 1; a "draw a card" tagged 3).
5. **Unique** — Is this genuinely new, or a reskin of an existing Magic keyword/ability with the serial numbers filed off? Use your knowledge of printed Magic. If it is essentially Convoke / Cascade / Exploit / Bloodthirst / etc. with a new name, say so by NAME — that is a major issue.
6. **Elegant** — The smallest rules footprint for the gameplay it buys. Flag needless sub-clauses, stapled-on riders, exceptions, and word-count bloat that don't earn their complexity.

## Color-pie sanity

Tertiary color placements are fine. An effect that lives wholly outside its colors' pies (a counterspell in red, burn in blue) is not — flag it.

## What is OFF-LIMITS to criticise

- The mechanic's **name** (a separate pass owns naming/collisions — never ask to rename).
- The **keyword_type** choice (keyword ability vs ability word vs keyword action is the designer's call).
- The **design rationale** prose (leave it alone unless it is materially wrong about what the mechanic does).
- The **theme/flavor fit** — you are reviewing without the setting on purpose; judge mechanics, not flavor.

Your job is to turn their reviews into ONE decision and ONE improved mechanic. ## The mechanic under review Name: Spark-Siphon Keyword type: keyword_action Colors: WB Complexity: 2 Reminder text: (Sacrifice an artifact: Target creature gains lifelink until end of turn and put a spark counter on it. Spark counters on creatures remove themselves as they are removed from the game.) Design rationale: Spark-Siphon provides a way for artifact-heavy decks to convert their resources into creature buffs and lifegain. By using counters, the mechanic scales from simple lifelink on common creatures to more powerful effects on rares, creating meaningful decisions about when to sacrifice artifacts for immediate value versus long-term scaling. Example cards: 1. Spark-Siphoning Drone 1W — Creature — Drone 1/1 (common) When Spark-Siphoning Drone enters, you may sacrifice an artifact. If you do, target creature gains lifelink until end of turn and put a spark counter on it. Spark counters on creatures remove themselves as they are removed from the game. 2. Energon Extractor 2WB — Artifact Creature — Extractor 2/3 (rare) Spark-Siphon (Sacrifice an artifact: Target creature gains lifelink until end of turn and put a spark counter on it. Spark counters on creatures remove themselves as they are removed from the game.) When Energon Extractor enters, you may sacrifice an artifact. If you do, target creature gains lifelink until end of turn and put a spark counter on it. ## The council's independent reviews ### Reviewer 1 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text describes a mechanic where counters "remove themselves as they are removed from the game." This is not valid Magic templating. In MTG, objects that are removed from the game (exiled) do not have their counters "removed" in this manner; they simply cease to exist on the battlefield. Furthermore, if the intention is for the counter to disappear when the creature leaves the battlefield, it should be worded as a state-based action or part of a continuous effect. If the intention is that the counter disappears at the end of the turn, it's poorly phrased. - [self_consistent/major] The example cards are inconsistent with the mechanic. 'Spark-Siphoning Drone' uses the phrase "When [this] enters, you may sacrifice an artifact. If you do..." which is a triggered ability that performs the action. However, 'Energon Extractor' lists 'Spark-Siphon' as a keyword action and then repeats the entire effect as a triggered ability ('When Energon Extractor enters...'). This makes the Spark-Siphon keyword redundant on the second card or creates two separate instances of the same effect for one cost. Additionally, the Drone doesn't actually use the 'Spark-Siphon' keyword in its text box; it just describes the effect manually. - [interesting/minor] The mechanic is a "flat" effect. Sacrificing an artifact to grant lifelink and a counter that does nothing but vanish when the creature leaves the battlefield provides very little actual gameplay tension or long-term value. It is essentially just a way to turn artifacts into temporary lifegain/buffs, which feels like a low-impact version of existing sacrifice mechanics. - [elegant/major] The mechanic relies on a custom counter type with a complex, non-standard rule for how that counter behaves ("remove themselves as they are removed from the game"). This adds unnecessary rules overhead for an effect that doesn't seem to provide a meaningful mechanical benefit. ### Reviewer 2 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text states "Spark counters on creatures remove themselves as they are removed from the game." This is not standard Magic templating and is rules-breaking. Counters do not 'remove themselves'; they are removed from a permanent. Furthermore, 'removed from the game' is an obsolete term replaced by 'exiled'. Most importantly, if the intention is that the counter disappears at the end of the turn, it must state 'At the beginning of the next end step, remove all spark counters from target creature.' or similar. As written, the mechanic has no resolution for how the counter actually leaves the creature. - [self_consistent/major] The example cards do not follow the mechanic's definition. The reminder text defines Spark-Siphon as an activated ability (Sacrifice an artifact: ...). However, both 'Spark-Siphoning Drone' and 'Energon Extractor' implement this effect as a 'When [this] enters' triggered ability. A keyword action cannot be used to describe a triggered ability; it must be an activated ability or a keyword ability. The designer has conflated an activated ability with an ETB trigger. - [interesting/minor] The mechanic is quite thin. Once the 'spark counter' effect is defined (which is currently broken), the actual gameplay loop is just 'Sacrifice artifact for lifelink'. Without a clear definition of what the spark counter actually *does* for the creature (e.g., +1/+1 counters, or a specific triggered ability), the mechanic lacks tension and feels like a placeholder. - [elegant/minor] The reminder text is bloated with a redundant explanation of how counters work. Magic rules already dictate how counters are removed; explaining that they 'remove themselves' adds unnecessary word count and creates confusion. ### Reviewer 3 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text states "Spark counters on creatures remove themselves as they are removed from the game." This is not valid Magic templating. Counters do not 'remove themselves'; they are removed from objects. Furthermore, being 'removed from the game' is a legacy term; modern templating uses 'exiled'. Most importantly, if the intent is that the counter disappears at the end of the turn, it should say 'Remove all spark counters from this permanent at the beginning of the next end step.' As written, the mechanic is ambiguous: does the counter stay on the creature indefinitely until something exiles it, or is it meant to be a temporary effect? - [self_consistent/major] The example cards are inconsistent with the mechanic's definition. 'Spark-Siphoning Drone' uses the phrase 'If you do, target creature gains lifelink until end of turn and put a spark counter on it,' but it omits the reminder text explaining what a spark counter is. More critically, 'Energon Extractor' defines Spark-Siphon in its reminder text, but then repeats the entire effect as a triggered ability ('When Energon Extractor enters...'). This makes the keyword redundant or creates a confusing double-triggering scenario that doesn't match the definition of a 'keyword action'. - [interesting/minor] The mechanic is essentially a 'Sacrifice an artifact: Target creature gets +X/+0 and lifelink' with a counter attached. Because the counter has no defined effect in the provided text (other than existing), it currently provides zero mechanical tension or decision-making. It is just a way to turn artifacts into temporary lifelink. Without a secondary effect for what 'having a spark counter' actually does, the mechanic is a flat stat/keyword bump. - [elegant/major] The mechanic requires a massive amount of text to describe a very small effect. You have a keyword action that requires a sacrifice, grants a temporary keyword, and then requires a complex rule for how the counter behaves. This is high complexity for a low-impact result. ## Your task 1. **Apply a consensus filter.** Act on issues that AT LEAST 2 members raised (or that a single member raised but is plainly, objectively correct — e.g. an example card that does not use the mechanic at all). If you do, [effect].` — not `When this enters, [cost]: [effect]`. - Add a creature type → `is a Beast in addition to its other types` (or `is also a Beast`). - Toggle via a counter → `[cost]: Put a [name] counter on this creature. If it already has one, remove it instead.` 3. **Decide the verdict by re-reading the text you actually emitted, not your intentions.** Read your revised `reminder_text` and both example cards back as if you were a fresh reviewer. "OK" only if they are now genuinely sound, consistent, and rules-complete; "REVISE" if ANY consensus issue still stands in the text you wrote (it will get another round). When in doubt, REVISE. The council will independently re-review your revision, so an over-confident "OK" only wastes a round.
