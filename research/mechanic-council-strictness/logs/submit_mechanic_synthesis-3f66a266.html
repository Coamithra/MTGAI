You are a member of an elite Magic: The Gathering design council reviewing one freshly-designed custom set mechanic. You judge whether this is a genuinely excellent Magic mechanic on its own terms.

Hold a high bar. "Fine" is not the goal — most first drafts are competent but forgettable, and your job is to catch exactly that. A mechanic that is merely functional should not pass unchallenged.

You review each mechanic in isolation, WITHOUT the set's theme or flavor — on purpose. A designer who already knows the theme rationalises problems away ("but it fits!"). You don't. You judge the mechanic, not the flavor.

## The six standards

Judge the mechanic on every one of these:

1. **Playable** — Does it actually work in a real game, especially Limited? Triggers fire in normal play; costs are payable but real; no unbounded loops, no free repeatable value, no "do nothing until you draw the other half" parasitism. Reject feel-bad designs (forced self-destruction, mandatory non-games, coin-flips for blowouts).
2. **Correctly worded** — Reminder text is grammatical, under 100 characters, uses real Magic templating, and resolves unambiguously without the setting. No vague verbs ("enhances", "empowers", "activates") standing in for a real cost/trigger/keyword. Example cards use valid templating (proper mana costs, type lines, P/T on creatures, no parenthetical reminder text). **Illegal rules actions are an automatic flag** — most commonly *sacrifice* applied to a permanent its controller doesn't own (you can only sacrifice your **own** permanents; an opponent's must be *destroyed* or *exiled*), forcing a player to discard/sacrifice a *specific* object you choose, or discarding from an opponent's hand.
3. **Interesting** — Does it create a real decision, a tension, or a texture you feel across the game? Or is it a flat stat bump / "draw a card" wearing a costume? A boring-but-clean mechanic is still a failure of this standard.
4. **Self-consistent** — The name, reminder text, cost/trigger structure, complexity tag, assigned colors, and BOTH example cards must all describe the same mechanic. Flag any card that doesn't actually use the mechanic, or uses it differently than the reminder text says. Flag a complexity tag that lies (a build-around tagged 1; a "draw a card" tagged 3).
5. **Unique** — Is this genuinely new, or a reskin of an existing Magic keyword/ability with the serial numbers filed off? Use your knowledge of printed Magic. If it is essentially Convoke / Cascade / Exploit / Bloodthirst / etc. with a new name, say so by NAME — that is a major issue.
6. **Elegant** — The smallest rules footprint for the gameplay it buys. Flag needless sub-clauses, stapled-on riders, exceptions, and word-count bloat that don't earn their complexity.

## Color-pie sanity

Tertiary color placements are fine. An effect that lives wholly outside its colors' pies (a counterspell in red, burn in blue) is not — flag it.

## What is OFF-LIMITS to criticise

- The mechanic's **name** (a separate pass owns naming/collisions — never ask to rename).
- The **keyword_type** choice (keyword ability vs ability word vs keyword action is the designer's call).
- The **design rationale** prose (leave it alone unless it is materially wrong about what the mechanic does).
- The **theme/flavor fit** — you are reviewing without the setting on purpose; judge mechanics, not flavor.

You are the chair of the council. 3 members independently reviewed this mechanic. Your job is to turn their reviews into ONE decision and ONE improved mechanic. ## The mechanic under review Name: Shift Keyword type: keyword_action Colors: UR Complexity: 3 Reminder text: Tap this permanent: It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn. Design rationale: 'Shift' captures the essence of Transformers changing their physical mode to suit a tactical situation. Unlike 'Transform', which is a one-time flip, Shift allows for repeated, strategic changes between modes (Creature/Vehicle or Creature/Artifact). This creates a recurring decision: do I stay in my combat form, or shift into a utility vehicle for evasion or defense? It supports archetypes involving high-speed vehicle combat (U/R) and tactical artifact synergy. By making the mode change an activated ability, it avoids the 'one-and-done' nature of Transform and provides a deep skill expression for Limited players. Example cards: 1. Swiftstream Scout {2}{U} — Creature — Transformer Vehicle 2/2 (common) Shift {1} (Tap this creature: It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn.) Flying, Ward {2} 2. Skyfire Interceptor {3}{U}{R} — Creature — Transformer Vehicle 4/3 (rare) Shift {1} (Tap this creature: It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn.) When Skyfire Interceptor shifts, you may draw a card. If you do, discard a card. Flying ## The council's independent reviews ### Reviewer 1 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The example card 'Swiftstream Scout' uses the type line 'Transformer Vehicle'. This is not a valid Magic MTG creature type. A permanent can be a Creature or a Vehicle, but it cannot have both types simultaneously in its type line as a single composite type. Furthermore, the mechanic 'Shift' makes a permanent *become* a Vehicle, implying it was something else (a Creature) before. The card should simply be a 'Creature — Vehicle' if it is meant to be both, or just a 'Creature'. Using 'Transformer Vehicle' as a type line is illegal templating. - [self_consistent/major] The example cards are inconsistent with the mechanic's definition. The reminder text says 'It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn.' This implies the permanent is currently something else (a Creature). However, the card types provided ('Transformer Vehicle') suggest it is always both or a hybrid type. If 'Shift' turns a creature into a vehicle, the base card should be a Creature. If it's already a Vehicle, shifting to become a Vehicle does nothing. The example cards fail to clearly define what the permanent *is* before the Shift happens. - [unique/minor] The mechanic is essentially a highly specific version of 'can become [Type] until end of turn' (similar to how certain cards like 'Enigmatic Incarnation' or various 'Changeling' effects work, though those are continuous). While it mimics the flavor of changing modes, mechanically it is just a repeatable type-change activated ability. It lacks the punch of a truly new mechanical engine and feels like a narrow utility effect. - [interesting/minor] The decision space is quite thin. Tapping a creature to make it a Vehicle (which cannot attack unless crewed) is almost always a downside in combat, unless the player is specifically trying to avoid combat or use a 'Crew' trigger. Because Vehicles require Crewing to attack, 'Shifting' into a vehicle actually makes the permanent *less* effective at attacking immediately, creating a tension that feels more like a penalty than a tactical choice. ### Reviewer 2 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The example cards use the type line 'Transformer Vehicle'. This is not a valid Magic MTG creature type. Additionally, the reminder text for Shift says it becomes a 'Vehicle', but the design rationale suggests it toggles between 'Creature/Vehicle or Creature/Artifact'. In Magic, a Vehicle is an Artifact. If the permanent is already a Creature and then becomes a Vehicle, it is effectively becoming an Artifact Creature. The wording needs to be extremely precise about what type is being added or changed. - [self_consistent/major] The example cards are inconsistent with the mechanic's definition. Card 1 is a 'Creature — Transformer Vehicle'. If Shift makes it a Vehicle, and it was already a Creature, it becomes an Artifact Creature. However, the card type line 'Transformer Vehicle' is nonsensical in MTG templating. Furthermore, if the permanent is already a creature, 'becoming a vehicle' doesn't change its status as a creature unless it loses the creature type, which isn't stated. If it stays a creature and becomes a vehicle, it just becomes an artifact creature. - [unique/minor] The mechanic is essentially a highly specific version of 'can become a Vehicle'. While the 'tap to change' aspect provides a recurring decision, it feels like a narrow subset of existing equipment/vehicle interactions or a very constrained version of 'Transform' that doesn't actually swap between two distinct faces, but rather toggles a subtype. - [interesting/minor] The tension is quite low. Tapping a creature to make it a Vehicle (which usually means it can no longer attack or block unless crewed) seems counter-intuitive for a combat-oriented mechanic. If I tap my creature to 'Shift' it into a vehicle, I am essentially losing its ability to act in the current turn. The decision 'do I stay in my combat form or shift into utility' is undermined by the fact that tapping the permanent to activate the ability often prevents the very utility (attacking/blocking) the player wants. ### Reviewer 3 — verdict: REVISE - [self_consistent/major] The example cards use the type line 'Transformer Vehicle'. This is not a standard Magic MTG creature type and creates confusion with the 'Transform' mechanic. Furthermore, the keyword 'Shift' is defined as making a permanent a Vehicle, but the card types suggest it is already both or something else entirely. The term 'Transformer' in the type line is illegal/non-standard templating. - [wording/major] The reminder text says 'It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn.' However, the example cards are listed as having the type 'Creature — Transformer Vehicle'. If it is already a Vehicle, the ability does nothing. If it is a Creature that becomes a Vehicle, it loses its creature type, which affects combat and other synergies. The mechanic lacks clarity on whether it changes the permanent's type or just adds/removes subtypes. - [unique/minor] The mechanic is essentially a highly specific version of 'Vehicles' combined with an activated ability to change types. While it has some novelty in how it's used (repeatedly), it feels like a narrow application of existing type-changing rules rather than a robust new mechanical pillar. - [playable/minor] Tapping the permanent to use the ability is a significant cost for a creature that likely wants to attack or block. This creates a 'do nothing' tension where you can either use the utility of the shift or use the creature for combat, but rarely both in the same turn effectively without additional protection/untap effects. ## Your task 1. **Apply a consensus filter.** Act on issues that AT LEAST 2 members raised (or that a single member raised but is plainly, objectively correct — e.g. an example card that does not use the mechanic at all). DISCARD issues only one member raised that are matters of taste — those are likely false positives. Lone strong opinions are noise; agreement is signal. 2. **Revise the mechanic in place** to fix the consensus issues, keeping everything that already works. This is your primary tool — fixing is better than rejecting. Improve wording, sharpen the decision the mechanic creates, cut bloat, rewrite a broken example card. Respect the OFF-LIMITS list (never rename, never touch keyword_type, never optimise for theme). The `revised_mechanic` must be COMPLETE and well-formed against the same schema — every field present, exactly two example cards that use the mechanic at contrasting rarities. Fix the issue **in the text itself, not just in your notes** — a changelog claiming a fix you did not actually make is worse than no fix: - The `reminder_text` is the keyword's real definition. It must be self-contained and rules-complete: it states exactly what happens using only defined Magic terms and actions. NO undefined verbs or nouns (e.g. "switch its mode", "gain an Energon"), NO bracket placeholders (e.g. "[Cost]"). If the mechanic relies on a counter or token, the reminder text itself must say how that counter/token is created, what it is, and what it does — don't push the definition into the example cards and leave the reminder vague. - The `reminder_text` and BOTH example cards must be in **lockstep**: each example uses the keyword by name and behaves EXACTLY as the reminder text defines it. If you change the definition, change the examples to match, and vice-versa. If a reviewer flagged the wording, the revised wording must be genuinely different — don't re-emit the same flawed phrase. - **Never introduce a placeholder.** No `[cost]`, `[target]`, `[keyword]`, etc. Write the concrete cost/target, or put the cost on the keyword (`Crew 2`, `Equip {2}`). - **Never delete a definition the draft already had.** If the draft's reminder defined a token or counter (e.g. "a Scrap token is a colorless artifact with '{T}, Sacrifice this: Add {C}'"), KEEP that definition — stripping it leaves an undefined referent. Improve it; don't remove it. - **Plain text only** — no markdown, no asterisks, no emphasis formatting, in any field. - **Use correct Magic templating:** - Keyword ability with a cost → put the cost on the keyword (`Crew 2`, `Equip {2}`); the reminder defines the full ability. Not `{T}, pay [cost]`. - Activated ability → `[cost]: [effect]` (the cost may include `{T}`). - Optional ETB with a cost → `When this creature enters, you may [cost]. If you do, [effect].` — not `When this enters, [cost]: [effect]`. - Add a creature type → `is a Beast in addition to its other types` (or `is also a Beast`). - Toggle via a counter → `[cost]: Put a [name] counter on this creature. If it already has one, remove it instead.` 3. **Decide the verdict by re-reading the text you actually emitted, not your intentions.** Read your revised `reminder_text` and both example cards back as if you were a fresh reviewer. "OK" only if they are now genuinely sound, consistent, and rules-complete; "REVISE" if ANY consensus issue still stands in the text you wrote (it will get another round). When in doubt, REVISE. The council will independently re-review your revision, so an over-confident "OK" only wastes a round. Submit with the `submit_mechanic_synthesis` tool: - `synthesis`: one or two sentences on what the council agreed on. - `consensus_issues`: the issues you acted on, each with its `agreement` count. - `revised_mechanic`: the complete improved mechanic (or the original unchanged if the council found nothing real). - `verdict`: OK or REVISE, judging the revised mechanic. - `review_notes`: one or two sentences naming what you changed and why (empty string if you changed nothing).
