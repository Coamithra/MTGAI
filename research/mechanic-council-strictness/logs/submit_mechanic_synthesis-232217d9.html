You are a member of an elite Magic: The Gathering design council reviewing one freshly-designed custom set mechanic. You judge whether this is a genuinely excellent Magic mechanic on its own terms.

Hold a high bar. "Fine" is not the goal — most first drafts are competent but forgettable, and your job is to catch exactly that. A mechanic that is merely functional should not pass unchallenged.

You review each mechanic in isolation, WITHOUT the set's theme or flavor — on purpose. A designer who already knows the theme rationalises problems away ("but it fits!"). You don't. You judge the mechanic, not the flavor.

## The six standards

Judge the mechanic on every one of these:

1. **Playable** — Does it actually work in a real game, especially Limited? Triggers fire in normal play; costs are payable but real; no unbounded loops, no free repeatable value, no "do nothing until you draw the other half" parasitism. Reject feel-bad designs (forced self-destruction, mandatory non-games, coin-flips for blowouts).
2. **Correctly worded** — Reminder text is grammatical, under 100 characters, uses real Magic templating, and resolves unambiguously without the setting. No vague verbs ("enhances", "empowers", "activates") standing in for a real cost/trigger/keyword. Example cards use valid templating (proper mana costs, type lines, P/T on creatures, no parenthetical reminder text). **Illegal rules actions are an automatic flag** — most commonly *sacrifice* applied to a permanent its controller doesn't own (you can only sacrifice your **own** permanents; an opponent's must be *destroyed* or *exiled*), forcing a player to discard/sacrifice a *specific* object you choose, or discarding from an opponent's hand.
3. **Interesting** — Does it create a real decision, a tension, or a texture you feel across the game? Or is it a flat stat bump / "draw a card" wearing a costume? A boring-but-clean mechanic is still a failure of this standard.
4. **Self-consistent** — The name, reminder text, cost/trigger structure, complexity tag, assigned colors, and BOTH example cards must all describe the same mechanic. Flag any card that doesn't actually use the mechanic, or uses it differently than the reminder text says. Flag a complexity tag that lies (a build-around tagged 1; a "draw a card" tagged 3).
5. **Unique** — Is this genuinely new, or a reskin of an existing Magic keyword/ability with the serial numbers filed off? Use your knowledge of printed Magic. If it is essentially Convoke / Cascade / Exploit / Bloodthirst / etc. with a new name, say so by NAME — that is a major issue.
6. **Elegant** — The smallest rules footprint for the gameplay it buys. Flag needless sub-clauses, stapled-on riders, exceptions, and word-count bloat that don't earn their complexity.

## Color-pie sanity

Tertiary color placements are fine. An effect that lives wholly outside its colors' pies (a counterspell in red, burn in blue) is not — flag it.

## What is OFF-LIMITS to criticise

- The mechanic's **name** (a separate pass owns naming/collisions — never ask to rename).
- The **keyword_type** choice (keyword ability vs ability word vs keyword action is the designer's call).
- The **design rationale** prose (leave it alone unless it is materially wrong about what the mechanic does).
- The **theme/flavor fit** — you are reviewing without the setting on purpose; judge mechanics, not flavor.

Your job is to turn their reviews into ONE decision and ONE improved mechanic. ## The mechanic under review Name: Shift Keyword type: keyword_action Colors: UR Complexity: 3 Reminder text: Tap this creature: It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn. Untap it. Design rationale: Shift allows a permanent to change its fundamental nature between combat and utility modes. By toggling between Creature and Vehicle types and providing an untap effect, it creates a meaningful decision about whether to use the permanent for immediate combat or for long-term artifact/vehicle synergy. Example cards: 1. Swiftstream Scout {2}{U} — Creature — Scout 2/2 (common) Shift {1} (Tap this creature: It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn. Untap it.) Flying, Ward {2} 2. Skyfire Interceptor {3}{U}{R} — Creature — Pilot 4/3 (rare) Shift {1} (Tap this creature: It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn. Untap it.) When Skyfire Interceptor shifts, you may draw a card. If you do, discard a card. Flying ## The council's independent reviews ### Reviewer 1 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text "It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn" is fundamentally broken because Vehicles are not creatures by default; they require a Pilot to be activated. If the creature becomes a Vehicle, it loses its ability to be tapped (since it's no longer a creature) and cannot be used as a source for most effects unless it has a Pilot. More importantly, if it becomes a Vehicle, it is no longer a creature, meaning you cannot tap it again or use it for combat unless a Pilot is present. The mechanic essentially turns a creature into a non-creature artifact, which makes the "Untap it" clause nonsensical because you can't tap something that isn't a creature. - [playable/major] The mechanic creates a "non-game" or "dead card" state. If a creature shifts into a Vehicle, it is no longer a creature. Unless the player has a Pilot, the permanent becomes an inanimate artifact that does nothing. This makes the mechanic highly situational and potentially detrimental to the player's board presence. - [unique/minor] The effect of changing types is not entirely new (e.g., Changeling or various 'become a [type]' effects), but the specific implementation here is muddled by the Vehicle interaction. - [interesting/major] Because shifting turns the creature into a non-creature, it doesn't create "tension" between combat and utility; it creates a choice between having a creature on board or having an artifact that cannot attack without a Pilot. This is a massive downside that isn't addressed in the design rationale. ### Reviewer 2 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text "It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn" is incomplete and creates a rules nightmare. A Vehicle requires a Pilot to be crewed. If the creature becomes a Vehicle, it ceases to be a Creature (unless specified otherwise), meaning it can no longer 'be' a Pilot or have creature-based abilities. More importantly, if it becomes a Vehicle, how does it get Crewed? If the player taps it to Shift, they are using its tap ability, but a Vehicle needs to be crewed by a creature to attack/block. This creates a logical loop: you tap it to make it a vehicle, but then it's no longer a creature that can crew itself or other vehicles. It also doesn't specify if it remains an artifact. - [unique/major] The mechanic is essentially a "pseudo-transformation" effect. While the 'untap' part is unique for a type-changer, the core loop of changing types to bypass combat restrictions or trigger synergies is something seen in various forms (like 'Manifest', 'Transform', or 'Reconfigure'). As written, it feels like an awkward attempt to bridge Creature and Vehicle types that doesn't quite land because Vehicles are fundamentally designed to be crewed by creatures, not to *be* the creature themselves. - [playable/major] The mechanic is highly problematic in Limited. If a creature becomes a Vehicle, it loses its ability to block (unless it has a way to stay a creature) and requires a Pilot. If 'Shift' is used on a creature that isn't already an artifact, does it become an Artifact - Vehicle? If so, the card is essentially just a creature with a very expensive tap ability that turns it into a non-creature. This is often a downside in Magic (losing combat presence) rather than a benefit, unless there is massive vehicle synergy. - [elegant/minor] The mechanic requires too much mental bookkeeping. "Tap -> Change Type -> Untap" is a lot of state-tracking for a single activation, especially when the resulting type (Vehicle) has its own complex rules regarding Crewing and attacking. ### Reviewer 3 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text "It becomes a Vehicle until end of turn" is incomplete. A Vehicle requires a Crew cost to be activated as a creature. Without specifying that it gains 'Crew {X}' or similar, the mechanic effectively turns the creature into a non-creature artifact that cannot be used for combat unless another effect provides Crew. If the intention is that it becomes a creature that can be crewed, it needs to specify how it's piloted. Furthermore, if it becomes a Vehicle, it loses its 'Creature' type, meaning it can no longer be tapped/untapped as a creature by its own ability after the first activation unless it has another way to become a creature again. - [unique/minor] The mechanic is essentially a highly specific, flavored version of 'can become a Vehicle'. However, because Vehicles are typically artifacts that aren't creatures, turning a creature into a vehicle usually makes it *less* useful in combat unless you have a way to crew it. It feels like a "downgrade" rather than a "shift". - [playable/major] In Limited, this is a very poor mechanic. Tapping a creature to turn it into a non-creature (Vehicle) that requires another creature to crew it is almost always a net loss in tempo and board presence. It creates a "do nothing" or "lose a blocker" situation rather than a meaningful tactical choice. - [self_consistent/minor] The second example card 'Skyfire Interceptor' has the ability 'When Skyfire Interceptor shifts...'. Since Shift is defined as a keyword action (an activated ability), it should technically trigger on the activation of the ability.
