You are a member of an elite Magic: The Gathering design council reviewing one freshly-designed custom set mechanic. You judge whether this is a genuinely excellent Magic mechanic on its own terms.

Hold a high bar. "Fine" is not the goal — most first drafts are competent but forgettable, and your job is to catch exactly that. A mechanic that is merely functional should not pass unchallenged.

You review each mechanic in isolation, WITHOUT the set's theme or flavor — on purpose. A designer who already knows the theme rationalises problems away ("but it fits!"). You don't. You judge the mechanic, not the flavor.

## The six standards

Judge the mechanic on every one of these:

1. **Playable** — Does it actually work in a real game, especially Limited? Triggers fire in normal play; costs are payable but real; no unbounded loops, no free repeatable value, no "do nothing until you draw the other half" parasitism. Reject feel-bad designs (forced self-destruction, mandatory non-games, coin-flips for blowouts).
2. **Correctly worded** — Reminder text is grammatical, under 100 characters, uses real Magic templating, and resolves unambiguously without the setting. No vague verbs ("enhances", "empowers", "activates") standing in for a real cost/trigger/keyword. Example cards use valid templating (proper mana costs, type lines, P/T on creatures, no parenthetical reminder text). **Illegal rules actions are an automatic flag** — most commonly *sacrifice* applied to a permanent its controller doesn't own (you can only sacrifice your **own** permanents; an opponent's must be *destroyed* or *exiled*), forcing a player to discard/sacrifice a *specific* object you choose, or discarding from an opponent's hand.
3. **Interesting** — Does it create a real decision, a tension, or a texture you feel across the game? Or is it a flat stat bump / "draw a card" wearing a costume? A boring-but-clean mechanic is still a failure of this standard.
4. **Self-consistent** — The name, reminder text, cost/trigger structure, complexity tag, assigned colors, and BOTH example cards must all describe the same mechanic. Flag any card that doesn't actually use the mechanic, or uses it differently than the reminder text says. Flag a complexity tag that lies (a build-around tagged 1; a "draw a card" tagged 3).
5. **Unique** — Is this genuinely new, or a reskin of an existing Magic keyword/ability with the serial numbers filed off? Use your knowledge of printed Magic. If it is essentially Convoke / Cascade / Exploit / Bloodthirst / etc. with a new name, say so by NAME — that is a major issue.
6. **Elegant** — The smallest rules footprint for the gameplay it buys. Flag needless sub-clauses, stapled-on riders, exceptions, and word-count bloat that don't earn their complexity.

## Color-pie sanity

Tertiary color placements are fine. An effect that lives wholly outside its colors' pies (a counterspell in red, burn in blue) is not — flag it.

## What is OFF-LIMITS to criticise

- The mechanic's **name** (a separate pass owns naming/collisions — never ask to rename).
- The **keyword_type** choice (keyword ability vs ability word vs keyword action is the designer's call).
- The **design rationale** prose (leave it alone unless it is materially wrong about what the mechanic does).
- The **theme/flavor fit** — you are reviewing without the setting on purpose; judge mechanics, not flavor.

You are the chair of the council. 3 members independently reviewed this mechanic. Your job is to turn their reviews into ONE decision and ONE improved mechanic. ## The mechanic under review Name: Overdrive Keyword type: keyword_ability Colors: BR Complexity: 2 Reminder text: (Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, create [number] 1/1 black Decepticon creature tokens. You may sacrifice a Decepticon creature. If you do, this creature deals that much damage to target creature an opponent controls.) Design rationale: Overdrive rewards aggressive combat momentum by scaling the number of tokens created based on the keyword value, while providing a way to convert those tokens into immediate board presence or removal through an optional sacrifice trigger. Example cards: 1. Decepticon Scout {1}{B} — Creature — Transformer 2/1 (common) Overdrive 1 (Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, create one 1/1 black Decepticon creature token.) 2. Megatron, Tyrant of Cybertron {3}{B}{R} — Creature — Transformer Decepticon Leader 5/4 (rare) Trample, Overdrive 2 (Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, create two 1/1 black Decepticon creature tokens. You may sacrifice a Decepticon creature. If you do, Megatron deals that much damage to target creature an opponent controls.) ## The council's independent reviews ### Reviewer 1 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text is fundamentally broken/ambiguous regarding the timing of the sacrifice. As written ('You may sacrifice...'), it reads like a triggered ability or a replacement effect that happens immediately upon combat damage. However, 'Megatron's' example implies this is part of the same trigger as creating the tokens. If it is a single trigger, it must be worded clearly to indicate when the choice is made. Furthermore, if the sacrifice is meant to happen *after* combat damage is dealt, it needs to be clear whether the creature can use its own Overdrive value or if it's a triggered ability that happens later. - [self_consistent/major] The reminder text and the example cards are inconsistent regarding what 'this much damage' refers to. In the reminder text, it says 'this creature deals that much damage', implying the Overdrive value (the number of tokens). However, in Megatron's card, it is unclear if 'that much damage' refers to the number of tokens created (2) or the power of the sacrificed Decepticon. If it refers to the token count, it's a very weak removal effect for a 5-mana creature. If it refers to the power of the token, the wording is incorrect. - [playable/major] The mechanic creates a 'do nothing until you draw the other half' problem or a massive value engine that is hard to balance. In Limited, if an Overdrive creature survives combat and hits a player, it generates tokens. If those tokens are then sacrificed to deal damage, the board state becomes extremely swingy. More importantly, as written, there is no way to use the 'Overdrive' effect *without* hitting a player, which makes these creatures highly susceptible to being blocked by a single 1/1, rendering the entire mechanic useless for several turns. This creates a high level of variance and potential non-games in Limited. - [unique/minor] The mechanic is essentially a combination of 'Enrage' (but on combat damage) and a token generator with a sacrifice outlet. While the specific combination is new, it feels like a collection of existing tropes rather than a cohesive new way to play Magic. - [elegant/major] The mechanic is too 'wordy' for a keyword ability. It tries to do three things at once: trigger on combat damage, create tokens, and provide a sacrifice-based removal outlet. This makes the complexity much higher than a standard keyword. A cleaner design would separate the token generation from the sacrifice effect. ### Reviewer 2 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text describes a triggered ability ('Whenever this creature deals combat damage...'), but the example cards present it as a static value/keyword ('Overdrive 1'). In Magic, if an effect is meant to trigger on combat damage, it must be written as a triggered ability. The current templating 'Overdrive X' implies a keyword with a value, but the text inside the parentheses defines a complex sequence of events that includes an optional activated-style ability. This creates ambiguity: is Overdrive a triggered ability that *also* grants an activated ability, or is it just a trigger? Additionally, the reminder text says 'You may sacrifice a Decepticon creature', but does not specify if this can be done at any time or only when the trigger resolves. - [self_consistent/major] The example cards do not match the reminder text's complexity. The reminder text includes an optional sacrifice effect ('You may sacrifice a Decepticon creature...'), but the example card 'Decepticon Scout' only includes the token creation part of the text in its parenthesis. This means the mechanic is defined differently on a common than it is on a rare, which is a violation of self-consistency. - [elegant/major] The mechanic is trying to do too much within a single trigger. It is combining a 'token generator' with an 'activated ability' (or a triggered ability with an optional effect). This creates a massive rules footprint for a complexity 2 mechanic. A player has to resolve the combat damage trigger, create tokens, and then immediately decide whether to sacrifice one of those new tokens or an existing one. It is a 'stapled-on rider' design where the removal effect feels tacked onto the token generation. - [interesting/minor] The mechanic lacks real tension. Because the tokens are created *by* the trigger and then can be sacrificed *during* the same trigger, it functions as a simple 'damage + tokens' or 'damage + removal' engine. It doesn't force a difficult choice between long-term board state (keeping tokens) and immediate removal; it just provides both options in one go. ### Reviewer 3 — verdict: REVISE - [wording/major] The reminder text for Overdrive is fundamentally broken because the sacrifice ability is a triggered ability (implied by 'You may...') but it lacks a trigger condition. As written, it reads like an activated ability without a cost, or a triggered ability that triggers on itself immediately upon token creation, which creates a loop of ambiguity. If it's meant to be an activated ability, it needs a cost (e.g., '{T}, Sacrifice a Decepticon...'). If it is part of the combat damage trigger, it must be phrased as 'When this creature deals combat damage to a player, create [X] 1/1 black Decepticon tokens. Then you may sacrifice...' - [self_consistent/major] The example cards do not match the reminder text. The reminder text says "create [number]... tokens", but the example card 'Decepticon Scout' uses 'Overdrive 1' and then defines it in parentheses as creating *one* token. This implies Overdrive is a keyword with a value, but the reminder text treats '[number]' as a variable. More importantly, the Megatron card includes the sacrifice clause in its definition of Overdrive 2, but the Scout card does not include the sacrifice clause in its definition of Overdrive 1. This means 'Overdrive' is not a single consistent mechanic; it is two different abilities depending on the number assigned to it. - [elegant/minor] The mechanic is overly wordy for a complexity 2 keyword. It attempts to combine a token generator with a removal engine into a single keyword, which creates a massive rules footprint for every instance of the ability. - [interesting/minor] As a design, this is essentially 'Combat Damage -> Token Generator' plus an optional 'Token Sacrifice -> Ping' effect. It feels like a cluttered version of existing 'Enters the Battlefield' or 'Combat Damage' triggers and lacks a unique mechanical identity beyond being a stat-to-value conversion. ## Your task 1. **Apply a consensus filter.** Act on issues that AT LEAST 2 members raised (or that a single member raised but is plainly, objectively correct — e.g. an example card that does not use the mechanic at all). DISCARD issues only one member raised that are matters of taste — those are likely false positives. Lone strong opinions are noise; agreement is signal. 2. **Revise the mechanic in place** to fix the consensus issues, keeping everything that already works. This is your primary tool — fixing is better than rejecting. Improve wording, sharpen the decision the mechanic creates, cut bloat, rewrite a broken example card. Respect the OFF-LIMITS list (never rename, never touch keyword_type, never optimise for theme). The `revised_mechanic` must be COMPLETE and well-formed against the same schema — every field present, exactly two example cards that use the mechanic at contrasting rarities. Fix the issue **in the text itself, not just in your notes** — a changelog claiming a fix you did not actually make is worse than no fix: - The `reminder_text` is the keyword's real definition. It must be self-contained and rules-complete: it states exactly what happens using only defined Magic terms and actions. NO undefined verbs or nouns (e.g. "switch its mode", "gain an Energon"), NO bracket placeholders (e.g. "[Cost]"). If the mechanic relies on a counter or token, the reminder text itself must say how that counter/token is created, what it is, and what it does — don't push the definition into the example cards and leave the reminder vague. - The `reminder_text` and BOTH example cards must be in **lockstep**: each example uses the keyword by name and behaves EXACTLY as the reminder text defines it. If you change the definition, change the examples to match, and vice-versa. If a reviewer flagged the wording, the revised wording must be genuinely different — don't re-emit the same flawed phrase. - **Never introduce a placeholder.** No `[cost]`, `[target]`, `[keyword]`, etc. Write the concrete cost/target, or put the cost on the keyword (`Crew 2`, `Equip {2}`). - **Never delete a definition the draft already had.** If the draft's reminder defined a token or counter (e.g. "a Scrap token is a colorless artifact with '{T}, Sacrifice this: Add {C}'"), KEEP that definition — stripping it leaves an undefined referent. Improve it; don't remove it. - **Plain text only** — no markdown, no asterisks, no emphasis formatting, in any field. - **Use correct Magic templating:** - Keyword ability with a cost → put the cost on the keyword (`Crew 2`, `Equip {2}`); the reminder defines the full ability. Not `{T}, pay [cost]`. - Activated ability → `[cost]: [effect]` (the cost may include `{T}`). - Optional ETB with a cost → `When this creature enters, you may [cost]. If you do, [effect].` — not `When this enters, [cost]: [effect]`. - Add a creature type → `is a Beast in addition to its other types` (or `is also a Beast`). - Toggle via a counter → `[cost]: Put a [name] counter on this creature. If it already has one, remove it instead.` 3. **Decide the verdict by re-reading the text you actually emitted, not your intentions.** Read your revised `reminder_text` and both example cards back as if you were a fresh reviewer. "OK" only if they are now genuinely sound, consistent, and rules-complete; "REVISE" if ANY consensus issue still stands in the text you wrote (it will get another round). When in doubt, REVISE. The council will independently re-review your revision, so an over-confident "OK" only wastes a round.
