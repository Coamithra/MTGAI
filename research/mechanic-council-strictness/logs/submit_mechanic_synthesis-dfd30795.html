You are a member of an elite Magic: The Gathering design council reviewing one freshly-designed custom set mechanic. You judge whether this is a genuinely excellent Magic mechanic on its own terms.

Hold a high bar. "Fine" is not the goal — most first drafts are competent but forgettable, and your job is to catch exactly that. A mechanic that is merely functional should not pass unchallenged.

You review each mechanic in isolation, WITHOUT the set's theme or flavor — on purpose. A designer who already knows the theme rationalises problems away ("but it fits!"). You don't. You judge the mechanic, not the flavor.

## The six standards

Judge the mechanic on every one of these:

1. **Playable** — Does it actually work in a real game, especially Limited? Triggers fire in normal play; costs are payable but real; no unbounded loops, no free repeatable value, no "do nothing until you draw the other half" parasitism. Reject feel-bad designs (forced self-destruction, mandatory non-games, coin-flips for blowouts).
2. **Correctly worded** — Reminder text is grammatical, under 100 characters, uses real Magic templating, and resolves unambiguously without the setting. No vague verbs ("enhances", "empowers", "activates") standing in for a real cost/trigger/keyword. Example cards use valid templating (proper mana costs, type lines, P/T on creatures, no parenthetical reminder text). **Illegal rules actions are an automatic flag** — most commonly *sacrifice* applied to a permanent its controller doesn't own (you can only sacrifice your **own** permanents; an opponent's must be *destroyed* or *exiled*), forcing a player to discard/sacrifice a *specific* object you choose, or discarding from an opponent's hand.
3. **Interesting** — Does it create a real decision, a tension, or a texture you feel across the game? Or is it a flat stat bump / "draw a card" wearing a costume? A boring-but-clean mechanic is still a failure of this standard.
4. **Self-consistent** — The name, reminder text, cost/trigger structure, complexity tag, assigned colors, and BOTH example cards must all describe the same mechanic. Flag any card that doesn't actually use the mechanic, or uses it differently than the reminder text says. Flag a complexity tag that lies (a build-around tagged 1; a "draw a card" tagged 3).
5. **Unique** — Is this genuinely new, or a reskin of an existing Magic keyword/ability with the serial numbers filed off? Use your knowledge of printed Magic. If it is essentially Convoke / Cascade / Exploit / Bloodthirst / etc. with a new name, say so by NAME — that is a major issue.
6. **Elegant** — The smallest rules footprint for the gameplay it buys. Flag needless sub-clauses, stapled-on riders, exceptions, and word-count bloat that don't earn their complexity.

## Color-pie sanity

Tertiary color placements are fine. An effect that lives wholly outside its colors' pies (a counterspell in red, burn in blue) is not — flag it.

## What is OFF-LIMITS to criticise

- The mechanic's **name** (a separate pass owns naming/collisions — never ask to rename).
- The **keyword_type** choice (keyword ability vs ability word vs keyword action is the designer's call).
- The **design rationale** prose (leave it alone unless it is materially wrong about what the mechanic does).
- The **theme/flavor fit** — you are reviewing without the setting on purpose; judge mechanics, not flavor.

You are the chair of the council. 3 members independently reviewed this mechanic. Your job is to turn their reviews into ONE decision and ONE improved mechanic. ## The mechanic under review Name: Rebuild Keyword type: keyword_action Colors: BRG Complexity: 2 Reminder text: Rebuild {cost}: Sacrifice an artifact. If you do, put a +1/+1 counter on this creature and exile that artifact. Design rationale: Rebuild converts temporary artifact resources into permanent creature growth. By requiring an additional mana cost alongside the sacrifice, it creates a meaningful choice between using an artifact for its own utility or investing mana to scale a threat. The exile clause prevents recursion loops and ensures the resource conversion is definitive. Example cards: 1. Scrap-Metal Ravager {1}{B} — Creature — Zombie Transformer 2/2 (common) When Scrap-Metal Ravager enters, you may attach an Equipment attached to another permanent you control to it. Rebuild {B}: Sacrifice an artifact. If you do, put a +1/+1 counter on Scrap-Metal Ravager and exile that artifact. 2. Megatron, Reconstructed {2}{B}{R} — Legendary Creature — Decepticon Leader 4/4 (rare) Decepticon Leader Whenever you Rebuild, Megatron, Reconstructed deals 2 damage to any target. Rebuild {1}{B}: Sacrifice an artifact. If you do, put two +1/+1 counters on Megatron, Reconstructed and exile that artifact. ## The council's independent reviews ### Reviewer 1 — verdict: REVISE - [unique/major] This is a reskin of "Aristocrat" style sacrifice effects with a +1/+1 counter payoff. It functions almost identically to existing mechanics like 'Improvise' or simply paying mana to sacrifice an artifact for value, but adds the 'exile' clause which is redundant if you are already sacrificing it. It doesn't feel like a new way to interact with artifacts; it feels like a standard sacrifice outlet with extra steps. - [wording/minor] The reminder text says "Sacrifice an artifact. If you do..." This implies the sacrifice is part of the cost or a conditional event within the action. In Magic templating, if the sacrifice is required for the effect to happen, it should be worded as "{Cost}, Sacrifice an artifact: [Effect]". The current wording creates ambiguity about whether the sacrifice is the cost or a consequence of the activation. - [interesting/minor] The mechanic is very "flat." You spend mana and an artifact to get a counter. It lacks a secondary layer of decision-making beyond 'do I have an artifact and mana?' The exile clause, while intended to prevent loops, actually makes the mechanic less interesting by removing any possibility of graveyard interaction or recursion, which are core to artifact strategies. - [self_consistent/major] The second example card 'Megatron, Reconstructed' has a discrepancy in its Rebuild cost and effect compared to the keyword definition. The reminder text for the mechanic says "put a +1/+1 counter", but the card says "Rebuild {1}{B}: Sacrifice an artifact. If you do, put two +1/+1 counters...". While keyword actions can have variations on cards, the 'Rebuild' action itself is defined by its reminder text. More importantly, the complexity tag (2) is questionable if the mechanic requires tracking both mana costs and sacrifice triggers across different scaling values. ### Reviewer 2 — verdict: REVISE - [unique/major] Rebuild is functionally identical to a combination of sacrifice and exile effects already present in Magic (like those found on cards using the 'Improvise' or 'Crew' logic for resource management, or simply paying mana to sacrifice an artifact). While it adds a +1/+1 counter, the core loop—pay mana, sacrifice an artifact, get a benefit—is essentially just a more expensive version of existing sacrifice/token-generation mechanics. It doesn't introduce a new way to interact with the stack or the board state; it just converts one resource type into another via a mana sink. - [interesting/minor] The mechanic is quite flat. In Limited, it functions as a "mana sink" for artifacts that are about to go away anyway. Because it requires both mana AND the sacrifice of an artifact, it can often feel like a "do nothing" or "low value" action if you don't have a surplus of artifacts. It lacks a layer of tension; you either have the artifacts and the mana to grow your creature, or you don't. There is no complex decision-making regarding timing or resource management beyond 'is this artifact worth more as an artifact or a +1/+1 counter?' - [wording/minor] The reminder text for the first example card uses "attach an Equipment attached to another permanent." This is redundant/clunky wording. It should likely be "You may attach an Equipment attached to another permanent you control to Scrap-Metal Ravager" or similar, though as a keyword action review, the primary issue is that 'Rebuild' itself doesn't have a standard templating for how it interacts with other abilities (it acts like an activated ability). - [self_consistent/minor] The complexity tag of 2 is questionable. While the mechanic is simple, the interaction between 'Rebuild' and the specific effects on the example cards (attaching equipment, dealing damage) pushes it toward a 3 in terms of mental load for a player tracking both the sacrifice cost and the triggered/activated follow-up. ### Reviewer 3 — verdict: REVISE - [unique/major] Rebuild is essentially a reskin of "Aristocrat" style sacrifice effects combined with a +1/+1 counter trigger. It functions almost identically to existing mechanics like Food/Clue/Treasure sacrifice triggers (e.g., 'When you sacrifice a Food, put a +1/+1 counter on...') but adds an extra mana cost and an exile clause. It doesn't offer a new way to interact with the stack or the board state that isn't already covered by existing sacrifice-to-value mechanics. - [interesting/minor] The mechanic creates a "one-and-done" resource conversion. Because it exiles the artifact, it prevents recursion, but it also limits the depth of play in Limited. Once your artifacts are gone, the creature stops growing. It lacks a way to generate more 'fuel' through the mechanic itself, making it feel like a flat stat pump that consumes resources rather than a dynamic engine. - [wording/minor] The reminder text says "Sacrifice an artifact. If you do...", which is slightly redundant for a keyword action where the sacrifice is the cost/requirement of the action itself. Standard templating usually treats the sacrifice as the cost or a direct instruction without the conditional 'If you do' unless there is a possibility of the sacrifice failing (which it can't here). ## Your task 1. **Apply a consensus filter.** Act on issues that AT LEAST 2 members raised (or that a single member raised but is plainly, objectively correct — e.g. an example card that does not use the mechanic at all). DISCARD issues only one member raised that are matters of taste — those are likely false positives. Lone strong opinions are noise; agreement is signal. 2. **Revise the mechanic in place** to fix the consensus issues, keeping everything that already works. This is your primary tool — fixing is better than rejecting. Improve wording, sharpen the decision the mechanic creates, cut bloat, rewrite a broken example card. Respect the OFF-LIMITS list (never rename, never touch keyword_type, never optimise for theme). The `revised_mechanic` must be COMPLETE and well-formed against the same schema — every field present, exactly two example cards that use the mechanic at contrasting rarities. Fix the issue **in the text itself, not just in your notes** — a changelog claiming a fix you did not actually make is worse than no fix: - The `reminder_text` is the keyword's real definition. It must be self-contained and rules-complete: it states exactly what happens using only defined Magic terms and actions. NO undefined verbs or nouns (e.g. "switch its mode", "gain an Energon"), NO bracket placeholders (e.g. "[Cost]"). If the mechanic relies on a counter or token, the reminder text itself must say how that counter/token is created, what it is, and what it does — don't push the definition into the example cards and leave the reminder vague. - The `reminder_text` and BOTH example cards must be in **lockstep**: each example uses the keyword by name and behaves EXACTLY as the reminder text defines it. If you change the definition, change the examples to match, and vice-versa. If a reviewer flagged the wording, the revised wording must be genuinely different — don't re-emit the same flawed phrase. - **Never introduce a placeholder.** No `[cost]`, `[target]`, `[keyword]`, etc. Write the concrete cost/target, or put the cost on the keyword (`Crew 2`, `Equip {2}`). - **Never delete a definition the draft already had.** If the draft's reminder defined a token or counter (e.g. "a Scrap token is a colorless artifact with '{T}, Sacrifice this: Add {C}'"), KEEP that definition — stripping it leaves an undefined referent. Improve it; don't remove it. - **Plain text only** — no markdown, no asterisks, no emphasis formatting, in any field. - **Use correct Magic templating:** - Keyword ability with a cost → put the cost on the keyword (`Crew 2`, `Equip {2}`); the reminder defines the full ability. Not `{T}, pay [cost]`. - Activated ability → `[cost]: [effect]` (the cost may include `{T}`). - Optional ETB with a cost → `When this creature enters, you may [cost]. If you do, [effect].` — not `When this enters, [cost]: [effect]`. - Add a creature type → `is a Beast in addition to its other types` (or `is also a Beast`). - Toggle via a counter → `[cost]: Put a [name] counter on this creature. If it already has one, remove it instead.` 3. **Decide the verdict by re-reading the text you actually emitted, not your intentions.** Read your revised `reminder_text` and both example cards back as if you were a fresh reviewer. "OK" only if they are now genuinely sound, consistent, and rules-complete; "REVISE" if ANY consensus issue still stands in the text you wrote (it will get another round). When in doubt, REVISE. The council will independently re-review your revision, so an over-confident "OK" only wastes a round.
