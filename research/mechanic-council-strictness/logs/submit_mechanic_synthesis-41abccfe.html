You are a member of an elite Magic: The Gathering design council reviewing one freshly-designed custom set mechanic. You judge whether this is a genuinely excellent Magic mechanic on its own terms.

Hold a high bar. "Fine" is not the goal — most first drafts are competent but forgettable, and your job is to catch exactly that. A mechanic that is merely functional should not pass unchallenged.

You review each mechanic in isolation, WITHOUT the set's theme or flavor — on purpose. A designer who already knows the theme rationalises problems away ("but it fits!"). You don't. You judge the mechanic, not the flavor.

## The six standards

Judge the mechanic on every one of these:

1. **Playable** — Does it actually work in a real game, especially Limited? Triggers fire in normal play; costs are payable but real; no unbounded loops, no free repeatable value, no "do nothing until you draw the other half" parasitism. Reject feel-bad designs (forced self-destruction, mandatory non-games, coin-flips for blowouts).
2. **Correctly worded** — Reminder text is grammatical, under 100 characters, uses real Magic templating, and resolves unambiguously without the setting. No vague verbs ("enhances", "empowers", "activates") standing in for a real cost/trigger/keyword. Example cards use valid templating (proper mana costs, type lines, P/T on creatures, no parenthetical reminder text). **Illegal rules actions are an automatic flag** — most commonly *sacrifice* applied to a permanent its controller doesn't own (you can only sacrifice your **own** permanents; an opponent's must be *destroyed* or *exiled*), forcing a player to discard/sacrifice a *specific* object you choose, or discarding from an opponent's hand.
3. **Interesting** — Does it create a real decision, a tension, or a texture you feel across the game? Or is it a flat stat bump / "draw a card" wearing a costume? A boring-but-clean mechanic is still a failure of this standard.
4. **Self-consistent** — The name, reminder text, cost/trigger structure, complexity tag, assigned colors, and BOTH example cards must all describe the same mechanic. Flag any card that doesn't actually use the mechanic, or uses it differently than the reminder text says. Flag a complexity tag that lies (a build-around tagged 1; a "draw a card" tagged 3).
5. **Unique** — Is this genuinely new, or a reskin of an existing Magic keyword/ability with the serial numbers filed off? Use your knowledge of printed Magic. If it is essentially Convoke / Cascade / Exploit / Bloodthirst / etc. with a new name, say so by NAME — that is a major issue.
6. **Elegant** — The smallest rules footprint for the gameplay it buys. Flag needless sub-clauses, stapled-on riders, exceptions, and word-count bloat that don't earn their complexity.

## Color-pie sanity

Tertiary color placements are fine. An effect that lives wholly outside its colors' pies (a counterspell in red, burn in blue) is not — flag it.

## What is OFF-LIMITS to criticise

- The mechanic's **name** (a separate pass owns naming/collisions — never ask to rename).
- The **keyword_type** choice (keyword ability vs ability word vs keyword action is the designer's call).
- The **design rationale** prose (leave it alone unless it is materially wrong about what the mechanic does).
- The **theme/flavor fit** — you are reviewing without the setting on purpose; judge mechanics, not flavor.

Your job is to turn their reviews into ONE decision and ONE improved mechanic. ## The mechanic under review Name: Ravage Keyword type: keyword_ability Colors: BR Complexity: 2 Reminder text: Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, you may put X Ravage counters on it. If you do, it deals X damage to that player. Design rationale: Ravage provides a scaling threat that rewards aggressive play by building up counters. By making the damage optional or providing ways to spend counters, it creates tension between immediate impact and long-term growth. Example cards: 1. Scraplet Swarm {1}{B} — Creature — BotBot 1/1 (common) Ravage 1 (Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, you may put a Ravage counter on it. If you do, it deals 1 damage to that player.) When Scraplet Swarm deals combat damage to a creature, you may remove a Ravage counter from it. If you do, destroy that creature. 2. Megatron, Tyrant of Kaon {2}{B}{R} — Legendary Creature — Transformer Decepticon 5/4 (rare) Ravage 2 (Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, you may put two Ravage counters on it. If you do, it deals 2 damage to that player.) Whenever Megatron, Tyrant of Kaon deals combat damage to a player, you may remove any number of Ravage counters from it. If you do, Megatron deals that much damage to that player. ## The council's independent reviews ### Reviewer 1 — verdict: REVISE - [self_consistent/major] The example cards use the mechanic inconsistently. Scraplet Swarm refers to removing a Ravage counter from 'it' (the creature), but the reminder text defines Ravage counters as being put on the creature that dealt damage. However, the second ability of Scraplet Swarm says 'remove a Ravage counter from it', which is grammatically ambiguous—does 'it' refer to the Swarm or the creature it dealt combat damage to? More importantly, Megatron's Ravage ability puts counters on *itself*, but its second ability allows removing them to deal damage. The Scraplet Swarm example uses the counter for a destruction effect on an opponent's creature, which is not supported by the definition of Ravage provided in the reminder text (which only describes putting counters on and dealing damage). - [wording/major] The reminder text 'Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, you may put X Ravage counters on it. If you do, it deals X damage to that player' is problematic for rules resolution. Because the damage is part of the same triggered ability/effect chain, if the creature is destroyed in combat or blocked by a creature that survives, the 'If you do' clause becomes ambiguous regarding whether the damage is dealt by the creature itself or as a replacement effect. Furthermore, putting counters on a creature and then immediately dealing damage creates a potential infinite loop or timing issue if not carefully worded. - [unique/minor] The mechanic is essentially a variation of 'Enrage' or 'Trample-lite' scaling effects combined with a 'ping' effect. While it has a unique flavor, the mechanical core (deal damage -> get a counter/do something) is very close to existing 'whenever this creature deals combat damage' triggers found in many sets. - [interesting/major] The mechanic lacks real tension because the Ravage ability as written is purely additive and positive. You put a counter on, you deal damage. There is no cost or risk mentioned in the keyword itself. The 'tension' mentioned in the design rationale only exists if the designer adds secondary abilities to cards (like Megatron), but the mechanic *itself* is just a scaling damage trigger. As a standalone mechanic, it's just a 'whenever this hits, deal X more damage' effect. - [playable/minor] In Limited, this could lead to 'non-games' where a single creature with Ravage grows so large that the opponent can no longer interact with it, or conversely, if the player cannot protect the creature, they have invested heavily in a single point of failure. ### Reviewer 2 — verdict: REVISE - [self_consistent/major] The example card 'Scraplet Swarm' uses the Ravage counter on a creature ('remove a Ravage counter from it. If you do, destroy that creature'), but the mechanic definition and reminder text only describe putting counters on the permanent itself to deal damage to a player. The interaction with creatures is not defined in the keyword. - [wording/minor] The reminder text 'Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, you may put X Ravage counters on it. If you do, it deals X damage to that player.' is slightly ambiguous regarding whether the damage is part of the same combat step or if it's a separate instance of damage. However, the primary issue is the lack of clarity on whether 'it' refers to the creature or the effect. - [unique/major] The mechanic is essentially a variation of 'Enrage' combined with a ping effect, but it functions as a scaling damage trigger. It feels like a reskin of existing 'whenever this deals damage' triggers found in various sets (like the 'ping' effects in newer designs), lacking a distinct mechanical identity beyond 'deal damage to player, get a counter'. - [interesting/minor] The decision-making is very thin. In most scenarios, if you are playing aggressive BR, you will always choose to take the damage now or save it for later. There is little tension unless there are specific ways to spend counters other than direct damage, which isn't part of the core mechanic itself. ### Reviewer 3 — verdict: REVISE - [self_consistent/major] The example card 'Scraplet Swarm' uses the Ravage counter on itself ('remove a Ravage counter from it'), but the mechanic text says you put counters ON the creature. While technically possible, the Scraplet Swarm ability refers to removing a counter from *it* (the swarm), whereas Megatron refers to removing counters from *himself*. However, the wording of the keyword implies the counters are on the creature itself. The real issue is that 'Scraplet Swarm' uses its own Ravage counter to destroy a creature, which is an effect not defined by the mechanic, and the interaction between 'dealing combat damage to a player' (to gain) and 'dealing combat damage to a creature' (to spend) creates a disjointed experience. - [wording/major] The reminder text is problematic: 'Whenever this creature deals combat damage to a player, you may put X Ravage counters on it. If you do, it deals X damage to that player.' This is an 'If/Then' trigger that essentially functions as a replacement effect or a triggered ability with a secondary effect. However, the phrasing 'it deals X damage' is ambiguous in Magic templating regarding whether this is part of the combat damage step or a separate event. More importantly, it creates a potential infinite loop or massive blowout if not carefully handled by the stack, though here it is an optional trigger. - [unique/major] The mechanic is essentially a reskin of 'Enrage' (from Ixalan) combined with a ping effect. Enrage triggers when a creature takes damage; Ravage triggers when a creature deals damage. It also functions similarly to 'Berserk' or various 'damage counters' mechanics seen in older sets. It doesn't feel like a distinct new way to play Magic, but rather a collection of existing tropes (counters + pinging) wrapped in one keyword. - [interesting/minor] The decision space is very thin. In Limited, you either take the damage to get the counter or you don't. Because the damage is optional and happens immediately, there is rarely a reason *not* to do it unless you are specifically trying to avoid a death blow, but since the creature is already attacking, that tension is minimal. It becomes a 'stat stick' mechanic where you just want to stack counters to win via burn. - [elegant/minor] The mechanic requires tracking a new type of counter (Ravage counters) and then provides two different ways to use them (one on the keyword itself, and one in secondary abilities). The council will independently re-review your revision, so an over-confident "OK" only wastes a round.
